## Definition
The Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L}$ is a function of the fields and their space/time derivatives
## Example
If we have a single field moving in one dimension of space $x$, then $u(x,t)\in \mathbb{R}$ is a field
In this case
$$
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}\left( u,\frac{ \partial u }{ \partial x } ,\frac{ \partial u }{ \partial t }  \right)
$$
Similarly for a regular lagrangian $L=L(q,\dot{q})$, we integrate to get an [[Action Principle|action]]: $S=\int L(q,\dot{q}) \, dt$, for a Lagrangian density,
$$
S[u]=\int \int \mathcal{L}\left( u,\frac{ \partial u }{ \partial x } ,\frac{ \partial u }{ \partial t }  \right) \, dt  \, dx 
$$
We have
$$
u_{t}=\frac{ \partial u(x,t) }{ \partial t } ,~u_{x}=\frac{ \partial u(x,t) }{ \partial x } 
$$
In the context of field theory, we do consider this to mean the standard definition of a partial derivative
