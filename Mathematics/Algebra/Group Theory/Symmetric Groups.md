## Definition
The symmetric [[Groups|group]] of degree $n$, $S_{n}$ is the group with elements which are [[Permutations|permutations]] of $n$ distinct symbols with group operation being the composition of permutations
$\left| S_{n} \right|=n!$ since in the first position we have $n$ choices of what to put, then the second we have $n-1$ etc
### Proof (of it being a group)
$\tau,\sigma:S\to S$ are [[Bijective Functions|bijections]], then $\tau \circ\sigma :S\overset{\sigma}\to S\overset{\tau}\to S$ is also a bijection
We have associativity as we already know composition of functions is associtive
We have the identity element:
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 & 3 & \dots & n \\
1 & 2 & 3 & \dots & n
\end{pmatrix}
$$
And we have inverses as every bijection has an inverse
Hence it is a group
## Remark
Finding the inverse:
$$
\sigma:i\mapsto\sigma(i),\sigma ^{-1}:\sigma(i)\mapsto i
$$
the way we do it is if we have
$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 & 3 & \dots & n \\
\sigma(1) & \sigma(2) & \sigma(3) & \dots & \sigma(n)
\end{pmatrix}

$$
The inverse is found by swapping the rows and then sorting the columns so that the top row is ordered

## Inverse of Cycles
If
$$
\sigma=\begin{pmatrix}
a_{1} & a_{2} & a_{3} & \dots & a_{k}
\end{pmatrix}
$$
$$
\sigma ^{-1}=\begin{pmatrix}
a_{k} & a_{k-1} & a_{k-3} & \dots & a_{1}
\end{pmatrix}
$$
So we can write the inverse of any permutation:
$$
\sigma=\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}\dots\sigma_{m}\implies \sigma ^{-1}=\sigma_{m}^{-1}\sigma_{m-1}^{-1}\dots\sigma_{1}^{-1}
$$

## [[Conjugate Elements|Conjugates]] in the Symmetric Group
Let $\sigma=\begin{pmatrix}a_{1} & a_{2} & \dots & a_{k}  \end{pmatrix}\in S_{n}$ be a cycle and let $\lambda$ be any permutation, then
$$
\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}
\lambda(a_{1}) & \lambda(a_{2}) & \dots & \lambda(a_{k})
\end{pmatrix}
$$
Hence importantly, the conjugate of a $k$-cycle is again a $k$-cycle
### Proof
Check $\lambda(a_{i})\to\lambda(a_{i+1})$
___

### Examples
Let $\sigma=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 4\end{pmatrix}\in S_{6}$ and $\lambda=\begin{pmatrix}2 & 5\end{pmatrix}$
By the proposition above,
$$
\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}
\lambda(1) & \lambda(2) & \lambda(4)
\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 5 & 4
\end{pmatrix}
$$
We can compute to check:
$$
\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}
 2 & 5 
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 & 4
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
5 & 2
\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 5 & 4
\end{pmatrix}
$$

## Cycle Types
Let $\sigma \in S_{n}$ be a product of disjoint cyces of lengths $n_{1}\leq n_{2}\leq\dots \leq n_{k}$ (including $1$-cycles)
The cycle type of $\sigma$ is $[n_{1},n_{2},\dots,n_{k}]$ and
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{k}n_{i}=n
$$

## Proposition
Two elements of $S_{n}$ are conjugate iff they have the same cycle type
### Proof
Consider $\sigma,\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1}\in S_{n}$, we want to prove they have the same cycle type
We write $\sigma$ as a product of $1$-cycles (including $1$-cycles):
$$
\sigma=\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}\dots\sigma_{k}
$$
Where each $\sigma_{i}$ is an $n_{i}$-cycle with $n_{1}\leq n_{2}\leq\dots \leq n_{k}$ with $\sum_{i=1}^{k}n_{i}=n$, then
$$
\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1}=\lambda\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}\dots\sigma_{k}\lambda ^{-1}
$$
$$
= \lambda \sigma_{1}\lambda ^{-1}\lambda\sigma_{2}\lambda ^{-1}\dots\lambda\sigma_{k}\lambda ^{-1}
$$
By lemma above, $\lambda \sigma_{i}\lambda ^{-1}$ is again a cycle of length $n_{i}$, in fact, all $\lambda\sigma_{i}\lambda ^{-1}$ are disjoint since $\lambda$ is a bijection 
Therefore, the cycle type of $\lambda\sigma\lambda^{-1}$ is the same as the cycle type of $\sigma$
For the converse direction, take $\sigma,\tau \in S_{n}$ of the same cycle type $[n_{1},n_{2},\dots,n_{k}]$, we need to find $\lambda$ such that $\tau=\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1}$
Let
$$
\sigma=\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2}\dots\sigma_{k},\tau=\tau_{1}\tau_{2}\dots \tau_{k}
$$
With
$$
\sigma_{i}=\begin{pmatrix}
a_{1}^{(i)} & a_{2}^{(i)} & \dots & a_{n_{i}}^{(i)}
\end{pmatrix},\tau_{i} = \begin{pmatrix}
b_{1}^{(i)} & b_{2}^{(i)} & \dots & b_{n_{i}}^{(i)}
\end{pmatrix}
$$
So define $\lambda \in S_{n}$ such that, for all $i=1\dots k$, $\lambda(a_{j}^{(i)})=b_{j}^{(i)}$ for all $j=1,\dots,n_{i}$
Thus $\tau=\lambda\sigma\lambda ^{-1}$
### Example
Lets construct the $\lambda$ defined in the last part of the proof for some $\sigma,\tau$:

\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}

 \tau=\begin{pmatrix}

\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}

\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 3
\end{pmatrix}\in S_{8}
$$
They both have cycle type $[2,3,3]$, we can write them thusly:
$$
\sigma=\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 7 
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 6 & 5
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
3 & 8 & 4
\end{pmatrix}
$$
$$
 \tau=\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 3
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 5 & 4
\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
6 & 8 & 7
\end{pmatrix}
$$
So we want $\lambda$ to be such that $1\to2,7\to 3,2\to 1,\dots$ to get
$$
\lambda=\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 & 8 \\
2 & 1 & 6 & 7 & 4 & 5 & 3 & 8
\end{pmatrix}
$$
