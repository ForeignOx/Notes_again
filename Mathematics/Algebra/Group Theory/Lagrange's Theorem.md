The [[Order of Elements|order]] of any [[Subgroups|subgroup]] of a finite [[Groups|group]] is a factor of the order of the group, i.e. $H\subseteq G$, then $\left| H \right|$ divides $\left| G \right|$
## Proof
Let element $a$ have order $k$
Then $<a> = \{ e,a,a^{2},\dots,a^{k-1} \}$
The size of the cyclic subgroup generated by $a$ is the same as the order of $a$, so it must be a factor of the order of the group
___
See the lemmas for [[Cosets|cosets]] for an alternate group
## Corollary
If $G$ is a finite group, and $g\in G$, then $\text{ord}(g)\mid \left| G \right|$
### Proof
For $g\in G$, consider the cyclic group $\left< g \right>$, $\left| \left< g \right> \right|=\text{ord}(g)$
By lagrange's theorem, $\left| \left<  g \right> \right| \mid \left| G \right|$, so $\text{ord}(g)\mid \left| G \right|$ 
## Example
Find all subgroups of [[Symmetric Groups|$S_{3}$]]. We know $\left| S_{3} \right| =3!=6$, so if $H\subseteq G$ is a subgroup, then $\left| H\right|\in\left\{ 1,2,3,6\right\}$
If $\left| H \right|=1$, then $H=\left\{ e \right\}$, if $\left| H \right|=6$, then $H=G=S_{3}$
If $\left| H \right|=2$, then $H=\left\{ e,x \right\}$ where $\text{ord}(x)=2$, so $x=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 2\end{pmatrix},\begin{pmatrix}3 & 2\end{pmatrix},\begin{pmatrix}1 & 3\end{pmatrix}$ 
So
$$
H=\left\{ e,\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2
\end{pmatrix} \right\}\text{ or }H=\left\{ e,\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 3
\end{pmatrix} \right\}\text{ or }H=\left\{ e,\begin{pmatrix}
1&3
\end{pmatrix} \right\}
$$
If $\left| H \right|=3$, then $H=\left\{ e,x,y \right\}$ and $\text{ord}(x)=\text{ord}(y)=3$, so $x,y$ are 3-cycles:
$$
\implies H=\left\{ e,\begin{pmatrix}
1&2&3
\end{pmatrix},\begin{pmatrix}
3&2&1
\end{pmatrix} \right\}
$$
